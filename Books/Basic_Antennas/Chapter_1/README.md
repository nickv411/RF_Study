# Chapter 1: Introduction to Antennas

A radio can be pictured similarly to a loudspeaker, but instead of hearing the sound, the frequency is above what your ear can pick up on.

If a magnet moves up and down near a wire, current will flow. If the magnet becomes stationary, it will no longer have current flowing. This is how electric generators transfer mechanical energy into electrical energy.

An **electromagnetic wave** is a wave consisting of a combination of both the electric and magnetic fields.

The **right hand rule** is a way to determine the direction of a magnetic field. In order to use it, you should hold a conduit of electricity with the negative pole up (the way the current is flowing), and then wrap your right hand around it with your thumb pointing up, the direction of the flow of electricity. Your fingers will loop in the direction the magnetic field will be rotating (counter-clockwise if looking from the top/negative end).

The only thing needed to make an electromagnetic wave is a change in current. The thing that generally keeps household wiring from emitting a magnetic field/electromagnetic field is the fact that household power is A/C, with the current alternating on two parallel wires, which has the effect of zero-ing out the magnetic fields/waves.

The frequency sent out as a radio wave starts with an electrical current that is changing at a particular frequency. Causing that current to flow through a conductor will result in an electromagnetic wave that varies at that frequency. If the signal is strong enough to reach a receiving antenna, a receiver tuned to that frequency will be able to receive that signal.

## Concepts to be familiar with

### Frequency

The rate at which an alternating current waveform or signal goes through a full cycle, from zero voltage to maximum, back to zero, to voltage minimum, and back to zero. The basic unit is hertz (Hz). A signal with the frequency of 1 Hz completes one cycle every second. Hz prefixes follow standard metric, Kilo, Mega, Giga, etc.

### Period

A period is defined as the time it takes for a waveform to make a complete cycle. The units are in seconds. If a waveform makes *F* cycles in one second, the time to complete a single cycle is *1/F*. This, a 3.6MHz signal would have a period of 1/3,600,000, which is 0.000000278 seconds. Therefore, the period would be 0.278 microseconds (insert fancy greek U here).

### Wavelength

In free space or the atmosphere, radio signals travel at the speed of light. Light travels at 300 million m/s. Using the above example, a 0.278 microsecond cycle period for a signal means that a cycle will cover 83.3 meters in that 1 cycle. That is the wavelength of a 3.6MHz signal. A wavelength is denoted by the Greek symbol lambda (λ). Thus, if something is two wavelengths it is *2λ*, or if it is half it is *λ/2*.
